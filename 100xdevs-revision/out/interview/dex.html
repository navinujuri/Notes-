DEXs, funding

Cohort 3.0 · 6 lessons · SDE-2 (3+ yrs) interview prep

Concept summary — all 6 steps

Web3 Cohort intro
Framing step positioning DEXs and token funding as the culmination of the DeFi curriculum. Interview-wise it's a no-op, but it signals the mental model: on-chain markets replace centralized order-matching and custody, and the rest of the topic builds toward launching and funding a token via a DEX.
Context: DEXs and funding
A DEX (decentralized exchange) is a set of smart contracts that let users swap assets peer-to-contract with no custodian or KYC; 'funding' here means projects raise capital by minting a token and bootstrapping a liquidity pool rather than a traditional raise. The key insight is that listing = creating a pool, and the pool's math (not a market maker or order book) sets the price.
Solana vs Ethereum
Ethereum is EVM/Solidity, account-based, ~12s slots, higher gas, but deepest liquidity, composability, and battle-tested security; Solana is Rust/accounts-model with Proof of History + parallel execution (Sealevel), ~400ms blocks, thousands of TPS, and cent-level fees. The trade-off interviewers probe: Solana's throughput/cost enables order-book DEXs (Serum/OpenBook) and consumer UX, while Ethereum wins on decentralization, tooling maturity, and TVL.
Recapping AMMs
An Automated Market Maker prices trades from a formula over pooled reserves instead of matching orders — Uniswap v2's constant product x*y=k is canonical, so price = y/x and every trade moves along the curve, producing slippage/price impact that grows with trade size relative to pool depth. Liquidity providers deposit both assets and earn swap fees; this is the mechanism a token launch uses to become tradable without a counterparty.
Impermanent loss
IL is the shortfall an LP suffers versus simply holding the two assets, caused by the AMM rebalancing reserves as prices diverge (arbitrageurs sell you the appreciating asset). It's 'impermanent' because it fully reverses if the price ratio returns to entry, and only realized on withdrawal; magnitude is fixed by the price ratio (~5.7% at 2x, ~25% at 10x) and is offset only by accrued trading fees — the core LP profitability question.
Creating a token
On Solana an SPL token is not a bespoke contract: you create a Mint account (decimals, supply, mint/freeze authority) owned by the shared Token Program, and balances live in per-user Associated Token Accounts (ATAs). On Ethereum an ERC-20 is its own contract implementing a standard interface (balanceOf, transfer, approve/transferFrom). Interview angle: revoking mint and freeze authority (Solana) or making supply immutable (ERC-20) is the trust signal that supply can't be inflated or accounts frozen.

Real-world example

Launching a project token and seeding a DEX liquidity pool

Your team wants to fund development by launching a utility token instead of doing a traditional raise. You mint an SPL token on Solana — a Mint account with 9 decimals and a capped supply — then revoke the mint authority so holders can trust supply is fixed. To make it tradable you seed an initial liquidity pool on an AMM DEX (e.g. Raydium/Orca) pairing your token with USDC; the constant-product curve, not a market maker, now sets the price, and the ratio you deposit is effectively the opening price. Weeks later early liquidity providers complain they'd have done better just holding the token, and you diagnose classic impermanent loss: as your token appreciated against USDC, arbitrageurs rebalanced the pool, leaving LPs underweight the winner — profitable only if accumulated swap fees exceed that divergence. You also had to defend choosing Solana over Ethereum in review: sub-second finality and cent-level fees suit a consumer-facing launch, accepting Ethereum's deeper liquidity and richer composability as the cost.

Interview Q&A (SDE-2)

Q1. Explain the constant product formula and how it determines price and slippage.
An x*y=k AMM keeps the product of the two reserves constant, so the spot price is simply reserve_y/reserve_x. A trade moves reserves along the hyperbola: buying token X shrinks its reserve and raises its price, and the executed (average) price is worse than the spot price — that gap is slippage/price impact. Impact scales with trade size relative to pool depth, which is why large swaps in shallow pools are expensive and why deep liquidity matters.
Q2. What is impermanent loss, when does it become permanent, and how large is it?
IL is the value gap between providing liquidity and just holding the same assets, arising because the AMM sells the appreciating asset and buys the depreciating one as arbitrage keeps the pool priced to market. It's 'impermanent' because it depends only on the current price ratio versus entry and fully disappears if the ratio returns — it becomes permanent only when you withdraw at a diverged ratio. For a 50/50 constant-product pool it's about 5.7% at a 2x move and ~25% at 10x, and LPs are net-positive only when collected fees exceed IL.
Q3. Compare Solana and Ethereum for building or hosting a DEX.
Ethereum offers the deepest liquidity, strongest decentralization, mature tooling, and composability, but ~12s finality and volatile gas make high-frequency, order-book-style trading impractical, pushing complex DEXs onto L2s. Solana's parallel execution and ~400ms blocks with sub-cent fees make on-chain central-limit order books and high-throughput AMMs viable (Serum/OpenBook, Raydium), at the cost of a smaller validator set, more historical downtime, and a steeper Rust/accounts programming model. The choice comes down to liquidity depth and security assurances (Ethereum) versus latency, throughput, and cost (Solana).
Q4. How does creating a token differ on Solana versus Ethereum?
On Ethereum every token is its own smart contract implementing the ERC-20 interface, so each deployment ships and maintains code. On Solana tokens share one audited Token Program: you create a Mint account holding metadata (decimals, supply, mint/freeze authorities) and balances live in per-owner Associated Token Accounts, so no custom code is needed for a standard token. This means Solana tokens are cheaper and safer to issue but less customizable, whereas ERC-20s can embed arbitrary logic (fees-on-transfer, hooks) at the cost of bespoke, exploit-prone code.
Q5. If impermanent loss is unavoidable, how do liquidity providers actually make money?
LPs earn a cut of every swap's trading fee (e.g. 0.3% on Uniswap v2) proportional to their pool share, plus any liquidity-mining/token incentives. Providing liquidity is profitable only when fee revenue plus incentives exceeds IL over the holding period, which favors high-volume pools and low-volatility/correlated pairs (like stablecoin pairs) where price divergence — and thus IL — stays small. It's effectively a bet that volume-driven fees beat the divergence cost.
Q6. What is the difference between an AMM and an order-book (CLOB) DEX, and why did order books appear on Solana?
An AMM prices trades algorithmically from pooled reserves with passive LPs and guaranteed liquidity at every price, whereas a CLOB matches discrete bid/ask limit orders posted by active makers, giving tighter spreads and price control but needing constant order updates. Order updates are cheap and fast only where fees and latency are low, so CLOB DEXs like Serum/OpenBook emerged on Solana, while Ethereum mainnet's gas costs made AMMs the dominant design. AMMs also enable permissionless listing — anyone can create a pool — which is why token launches use them.
Q7. What is concentrated liquidity (Uniswap v3) and how does it change the LP trade-off?
Instead of spreading liquidity across the entire 0-to-infinity price range like v2, v3 lets LPs concentrate capital within a chosen price band, so within that band the same capital provides far deeper liquidity and earns proportionally more fees (capital efficiency). The catch is amplified impermanent loss and the risk of the price leaving your range, at which point you hold 100% of one asset and earn zero fees. It effectively turns passive LPing into an active position-management problem resembling a range order.
Q8. Why do launches revoke mint and freeze authority, and what's the equivalent concern on Ethereum?
On Solana the mint authority can create new tokens (inflate supply) and the freeze authority can lock user token accounts, so revoking both is the on-chain proof that supply is fixed and balances can't be frozen — a key trust and rug-pull check for buyers. The Ethereum equivalent is verifying the ERC-20 has no owner-only mint() or blacklist/pause functions and that ownership is renounced or supply is immutable. In both cases due diligence is reading the authority/permission surface, not just the total supply number.
