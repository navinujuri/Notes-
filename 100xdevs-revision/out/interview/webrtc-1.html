WebRTC Basic implementation, advance discussion

Cohort 2.0 · 9 lessons · SDE-2 (3+ yrs) interview prep

Concept summary — all 9 steps

Why WebRTC?
WebRTC gives browsers/native apps sub-500ms, peer-to-peer real-time audio, video, and arbitrary data with no plugins and built-in encryption. The interview angle is knowing when to reach for it: use it for interactive/bidirectional media (calls, screen share, gaming, live collaboration) and NOT for one-to-many broadcast at scale where HLS/DASH's multi-second latency but CDN-friendly cacheability wins.
Architecture & jargon
Core pieces: RTCPeerConnection (the connection), MediaStreamTrack (audio/video), RTCDataChannel (arbitrary data over SCTP), SDP (capability description), ICE/STUN/TURN (NAT traversal), and a signaling server you provide. Interviewers probe whether you can name each component's job — especially that STUN discovers your public reflexive address and TURN relays media when a direct path is impossible.
Connecting the two sides
Connection setup is an out-of-band SDP offer/answer exchange plus ICE candidate trickling, both relayed through your signaling channel (usually WebSocket). Key insight: WebRTC deliberately does NOT specify signaling, so the app owns identity, rooms, and message transport; ICE then runs connectivity checks over gathered candidate pairs and promotes the best working path.
Implementation flow
The canonical sequence: getUserMedia -> new RTCPeerConnection(config) -> addTrack -> createOffer -> setLocalDescription -> signal offer; the remote sets it, createAnswer, and signals back, while both fire onicecandidate to exchange candidates and ontrack to render incoming media. Getting the ordering and event wiring right (and handling renegotiation) is what separates a demo from a robust integration.
Backend (signaling + TURN)
The backend is primarily a stateless-ish signaling relay (WebSocket) that manages rooms and forwards offers/answers/ICE candidates between peers, plus a coturn TURN/STUN deployment for NAT traversal. Media typically does NOT flow through this server in P2P mode — except through TURN — so scaling signaling and scaling media are separate concerns to reason about.
Frontend (browser APIs)
The frontend uses navigator.mediaDevices.getUserMedia/getDisplayMedia to capture tracks, wires RTCPeerConnection events, and attaches inbound MediaStreams to <video> srcObject. Practical gotchas interviewers like: HTTPS/secure-context requirement, autoplay + muted policies, permission handling, and cleaning up tracks/connections to avoid leaked camera indicators.
WebRTC stats & QoS
pc.getStats() returns an RTCStatsReport of live metrics — RTT, jitter, packetsLost, bytes/framesPerSecond, resolution, bitrate, and candidate-pair state. This is the primary tool for diagnosing bad calls and driving adaptive decisions; being able to say 'high jitter + rising packet loss on the inbound-rtp report points to congestion, not a NAT problem' demonstrates real operational depth.
Libraries for P2P
Libraries like simple-peer and PeerJS wrap the offer/answer/ICE boilerplate into a few callbacks, speeding up 1:1 builds; server-side, mediasoup/Janus/Pion/LiveKit provide SFU infrastructure. The trade-off interviewers want: convenience and fewer bugs vs. losing fine control over renegotiation, codecs, and simulcast — and the risk of an unmaintained abstraction hiding critical behavior.
Other architectures (mesh/SFU/MCU)
Mesh connects every peer to every other (O(n^2) uplinks) and collapses beyond ~4 participants; an SFU (Selective Forwarding Unit) has each peer send one upstream and forwards/selects streams (with simulcast) to others; an MCU mixes everything into one composited stream — light on client bandwidth but CPU-heavy on the server. The expected answer is choosing SFU for most group-call products and knowing the bandwidth/CPU/latency trade-offs of each.

Real-world example

Telehealth video consultation: 1:1 launch, a NAT failure, then group visits

You build a doctor-patient video consult feature. v1 is 1:1 P2P: a Node WebSocket signaling server pairs the two participants in a room and relays SDP offers/answers and trickled ICE candidates; the browser captures the camera via getUserMedia and renders the remote track via ontrack. It works on your laptop, but QA reports calls that connect then show frozen video for users on hospital corporate networks. getStats() shows ICE stuck with no succeeded candidate pair — the culprit is symmetric NAT/firewalls, so plain STUN can't punch through and you must deploy a coturn TURN server so media can relay. Once calls are stable, product asks for 3-4 person case reviews. You correctly reject a mesh (uplink and CPU blow up per added participant) and introduce an SFU (e.g., mediasoup/LiveKit): each client sends one upstream with simulcast layers, and the SFU forwards the appropriate resolution to each viewer. You wire getStats-based monitoring to flag high jitter/packet loss and downshift resolution on congested links. This single feature exercises the entire topic: why WebRTC, the SDP/ICE/STUN/TURN jargon, signaling, browser APIs, stats-driven debugging, and the mesh-vs-SFU architecture decision.

Interview Q&A (SDE-2)

Q1. STUN vs TURN — what does each do, and why is TURN a last resort?
STUN (Session Traversal Utilities for NAT) lets a peer discover its public IP:port (server-reflexive candidate) so two peers can attempt a direct hole-punched connection; the STUN server sees no media. TURN (Traversal Using Relays around NAT) is a media relay used when a direct path is impossible — typically symmetric NATs or restrictive firewalls. TURN is the fallback because ALL media flows through your server, consuming real bandwidth and cost, so ICE only promotes a relay candidate pair when direct/reflexive pairs fail.
Q2. Walk through the SDP offer/answer handshake and why signaling isn't part of WebRTC.
The offerer calls createOffer(), setLocalDescription(offer), and sends the SDP; the answerer setRemoteDescription(offer), createAnswer(), setLocalDescription(answer), and sends it back, after which both sides know each other's codecs, ICE parameters, and media lines. SDP is the negotiated description of capabilities. WebRTC intentionally leaves signaling transport unspecified so apps can reuse existing auth, presence, and infrastructure — most people use WebSocket, but it could be HTTP long-poll, SIP, or even copy-paste.
Q3. Mesh vs SFU vs MCU — how do you choose for a group video product?
Mesh has every participant send a stream directly to every other (O(n^2) uplinks and decode load), so it only survives tiny groups (~3-4). An SFU has each client send one upstream and the server selectively forwards streams — with simulcast it can send different resolutions to different receivers — giving the best balance of client cost and server scalability, so it's the default for most products. An MCU decodes and mixes everyone into a single composite stream, minimizing client bandwidth/decoders but incurring heavy server CPU and added latency; it's used for legacy interop or very constrained clients.
Q4. Why does WebRTC prefer UDP, and how is media secured and made resilient to loss?
Real-time media favors UDP because retransmitting late packets is worse than dropping them; head-of-line blocking from TCP would spike latency. WebRTC handles loss with codec-level concealment, NACK/retransmission for select frames, FEC, and jitter buffering rather than strict reliability. Media is always encrypted — DTLS handshakes to exchange keys and RTP is protected as SRTP — so encryption is mandatory, not optional; data channels run over SCTP-over-DTLS and can be configured ordered/reliable or unordered/unreliable.
Q5. How would you debug a call that connects but has terrible video quality?
Pull pc.getStats() and inspect the inbound/outbound-rtp and candidate-pair reports: rising packetsLost and jitter with adequate bandwidth points to network congestion or Wi-Fi loss; low framesPerSecond or frameHeight with high qualityLimitationReason='cpu' points to encode/decode pressure; a high currentRoundTripTime indicates a long/relayed path. I'd correlate whether the selected candidate pair is a TURN relay (added latency), check if bitrate is being capped, and then act — enable/tune simulcast, lower target resolution, or adjust bandwidth constraints — instead of guessing.
Q6. What is trickle ICE and why does it matter for connection time?
Without trickle, a peer gathers ALL ICE candidates (including slow-to-resolve relay candidates) before sending its SDP, adding seconds of setup delay. Trickle ICE sends each candidate via signaling as soon as it's discovered (onicecandidate) so connectivity checks start immediately in parallel with gathering. It's the standard approach and dramatically cuts time-to-first-frame; the remote side just adds candidates with addIceCandidate as they arrive.
Q7. What is the glare problem and how does 'perfect negotiation' solve it?
Glare happens when both peers try to create/send an offer simultaneously (e.g., both add a track at once), leaving the state machine in conflict. Perfect negotiation resolves it by designating one peer 'polite' and one 'impolite': on a colliding offer the polite peer rolls back its own local offer and accepts the remote one, while the impolite peer ignores the incoming offer and proceeds. This pattern, driven by onnegotiationneeded plus signalingState checks, gives robust, symmetric renegotiation logic without race conditions.
